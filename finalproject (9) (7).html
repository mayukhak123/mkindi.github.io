Global Suicide Data Analysis¶

In this project, we will explore global suicide data between 1985 and 2016. We will study and quantify the relations between suicide and its factors such as age, gender, and more.¶

Project by Mayukha Kindi¶

Introduction:¶

Suicide is one of the world's leading causes of death. Because suicidal thoughts can result from long-term underlying factors such as mental illness, stress, substance abuse, unsupportive housholds, and depression, the suicide epidemic has become difficult to address and solve (Mayo Clinic 2022). In an attempt to understand and tackle this epidemic, suicide prevention organizations are trying to identify trends in global suicide data. For example, if organizations can identify a certain group of people who are prone to suicidal thoughts, organizations can push politicians to allocate mental health resources towards this group. In this tutorial, we will analyze global suicide data from 1985 to 2016 using a Kaggle dataset, "Suicide Rates Overview 1985 to 2016". At the end of this tutorial, our goal is to make a suggestion on who politicians should allocate suicide prevention resources to.

Step 2 - Analysis Part 1: Year vs. Suicide Rate¶

In this section, we will explore how suicide rates have changed over time. We want to study if suicide rate has changed over time, so that if we observe a change, we can ask ourselves the following questions:

  • how can we visualize suicide numbers over time? how much do suicide rates change every year?
  • was there a year with peak suicide numbers? What happened in this year specifically that may have caused this?

In order to visualize suicide numbers over time, we can make a scatter plot of suicide numbers across time. We will use the suicides/100k population column specifically, so that we can standardize comparison between bigger and smaller countries. There are many ways you can make a scatter plot in python, such as with matplotlib or seaborn (these are two data visualization libraries in python). In this tutorial, we will use matplotlib, but I encourage you to also explore how to make a scatter plot in seaborn (https://seaborn.pydata.org/generated/seaborn.scatterplot.html). Matplotlib and seaborn have their own strengths, such as Matplotlib being compatible with more APIs and seaborn containing more advanced graphical features. For something basic like a scatter plot though, it doesn't matter very much what library you decide to use.

From this graph, we can see that over time, most countries's suicide rate was below about 150 for every 100,000 people. It is important to note though that many of these rates that did jump over 150 occured after 1990. Specifically, there are many rates closer to 200 between 2005 and 2012. What could've caused this? One hypothesis is that the Great Recession (which occured in 2007 to 2009) increased the rate of suicide as unemployment and poor financial circumstances often lead to stress which is a major factor of suicide. One thing to also note is that there does not seem to be a linear increase between the years. The bottom of the vertical bars tends to be constant, with occasional fluctuations on the top of the bars. Before we look more into this, let's find a clearer graph that shows the distribution and statistics of suicide rate per year.

A great way to do this is to plot a violin plot for each year. A violin plot will allow us to easily visualize the distribution shape of the data for each year, essentially providing us with per-year summaries of our data. While we could also plot a box plot to get summary statistics for each year, violin plots give even more information by also taking into account the density of the data point. Matplotlib provides an easy way to make this violin plot:

In order to plot our violin plot, we will pass two sets of data into the violin plot function. The data will take this form... year_1 => [suiciderate1, suiciderate2, suiciderate3...] year_2 => [suiciderate3,suiciderate4,suiciderate5...] . . . year_n => [suicideraten,suicideraten+1,suicideraten+2...] and we will pass in a list of years and their corresponding lists of suicide rates.

After creating the violin plot, we can observe the following.

In every year, we are unable to see a round violin shape. In other words, there's no curves, indicating there are no peaks in the suicide rate (no normal distribution). Why may this be? This could be because when there is a normal distribution, we definitely observe that there is a peak (meaning one datapoint that occurs in high frequency). This normal distribution may occur because it is expected/normal to have a certain datapoint peak. In this case, there's no "normal" or "center" suicide rate that all the other values surround.

We also notice the lines below 25 (suicide rate) are the bottom of the distribution for each year. The highest value in the distribution is the bar at the top. Between 1990 and 1995 and 2000 to 2010, there is a dramatic increase in the highest suicide rate compared to previous years. Additionally, between 2000 and 2005, we can see an almost linear increase in the highest value in the distribution. We can also see a linear decrease between the higher number of suicide rates between 2010 and 2015.

After some research (quick read: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_1990s_recession#:~:text=The%20early%201990s%20recession%20describes,Bush.), I was able to find that the early 1990's was also a short period of economic downfall for many people globally. This could be why suicide rates dramatically increased during this time. However, one rebuttal to this is that 2010-2015 is also known as a time of economic struggle (because many people remained unemployed after the recession), but suicide rates dramatically decreased between years.

While the graph we generated is somewhat useful, let's also try grouping the years together into different chunks, so we can see if we can find out anything else about the suicide rate distributions. Why is this neccesary? In statistical analysis, it is really important to think about the context we are working in. Typically, when people look at history, they think of different eras or periods of time (for example, instead of thinking about 2007, 2008, and 2009 individually, people often think about the overall Great Recession era which happened during those years). That's why it is also important for us to look at our data in the same way. Let's compare different eras together by assembling a new dictionary using the same techniques as earlier.

As we can see above, grouping the years together made a major difference in the distribution of the violin plots. For all of the distributions, we can see that the data is right skewed (most of the data leaning to the left). Why does the rate have this shape? Well, we can reasonably assume that suicide rate does not have a normal shape. This is because there isn't exactly a peak, but more like low suicide rates with occasional fluctuations that may result from countries with suicide epidemics. Our goal is to continue studying which demographics these high fluctuations come from.

Some positive insights from this graph:

  • Suicide does not seem to increase linearly over time (we can quantify this later using regression!). Look at the top bars of each violin plot, they do not follow any sort of consistent linear increasing or decreasing shape. The bottom bars of the violin plots also stay in the same place.
  • 1990-2000 had a relatively high suicide rate compared to the other time ranges.
  • Often, peaks of suicide rate were followed with decreases in suicide rate. Why is this? Potentially because of more discussion surrounding mental health after suicide epidemics.
  • Suicide rates tends to be on the lower side. Does that mean that most prevention policies currently are mostly effective or that they are being directed to the right crowds most of the time?
